## The Tale of Two Names

Somewhere around the 93rd parallel ‚Äî roughly the Iowa-Minnesota border ‚Äî a linguistic miracle occurs. The exact same dish, made with the exact same cream-of-something soup, the exact same frozen vegetables, and the exact same crunchy topping, changes its name.

Below the line: **casserole.**  
Above the line: **hotdish.**

Wars have been fought over less. (Midwesterners would never fight, of course. They'd just say "Oh, that's *interesting*" in a tone that means they've already judged you.)

## üèÜ The Official Hotdish vs. Casserole Judging Rubric

*Rate any baked-dish-in-a-pan on the following criteria. Maximum score: 50 points.*

### 1. Cream of Mushroom Soup Integration (10 points)
| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 10 | Perfectly incorporated. You can taste it in every bite but can't identify it. Grandma would approve. |
| 7-9 | Present and accounted for. Good soup-to-filling ratio. |

| 1-3 | Attempted a "from scratch" cream sauce. Respectable but missing the point. |
| 0 | No cream-of-anything soup. This is not a hotdish. This is a bake. Leave. |

### 2. Crunchy Topping Commitment (10 points)
| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|

## The Verdict

**There is no verdict.** A hotdish is a casserole is a hotdish. They are the same dish wearing different name tags at the same church potluck. 

The only real rule: Bring one. Doesn't matter what you call it. What matters is that you showed up, you brought food, and you're willing to stay and help clean up afterward.

*That's the most Midwestern sentence ever written, and we stand by it.*
